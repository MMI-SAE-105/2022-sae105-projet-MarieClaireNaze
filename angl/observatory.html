Eyes on space

The observatory Midi-Pyrénée (also called OMP) is the highest in France, above the clouds, 2 876 meters in altitude in the Haute-Pyrénées, enough to have stars in your eyes!

Created in 1870 by the general Charles du Bois de Nansouty and the engineer Célestin Vaussenat, this research center regroupe now 950 professionals: teachers-researchers, engineer, doctoral technicians and administrative personnel.

This former meteorological base, sadly abandoned during the wars, is now world-renowned because of the many research projects and discoveries made in astronomy, géology, astrophysics, ecology and in many other domains.

A light from past

Dear Traveler, do you know that we can see the past when we watch the sky?

In reality, between the moment where the star lights and when we see it in the Telescope there is always a certain amount of time.

This is because of the speed of light ( 300 000 meters per second): for stars close to us like the Sun this period lasts for just a few seconds but, like with Antares ( 500 light years away) it could be 600 years.

So, when you look at the stars you can see a light straight from the past!

Observatory, an old practice

the tools

At first, people during antiquity observed with nacked eyes and used an astrolabe, a tool which does many calculations.

In 1606, Hans Lippershey invented the first telescope which allowed us to see the different planets in the solar system. It used in 1609 by Gallilée to defend the Copernicus' theory of heliocentrism.

Later, many other scientists created their own telescope until 1666 when Isaac Newton created the first telescope with which one could see the deep sky.

L’histoire des observatoire

  • 1576: The Observatory on the Island of Ven

    The first known European observatory is built in 1576 by Tycho Brahe, a Danish astronomer. He decided to build it on the island of Ven, near Copenhagen. He finished the construction in 1584.

  • 1820: The South African Astronomical Observatory

    Also known as SAAO, this observatory in South Africa is the largest in Africa. It is also a resource and knowledge centre in multiple fields such as science, maths, communication and socio-economic development and aims to share knowledge and culture with as many people as possible.

  • 1870: the Pic du Midi Observatory

    As said before, the observatory of the Midi was a meteorological base at its creation, with many researches concerning the magnetism of the terrestrial poles, the radioactivity, the climate, the study of the atmosphere and the seism as well as the medicine. Now, other fields are being added to this, including astronomy.

  • 2013: Alma, the largest observatory in the world

    Located at 5100 meters above sea level in a desert in northern Chile, this observatory is the largest on the planet. It is an international project funded by the European observatories ESO, the US National Science Foundation, the Japanese National Institutes of Natural Sciences and the Academia Sinica in Taiwan. It is very well known to the general public for its sixty-six characteristic antennae that continuously observe space.
